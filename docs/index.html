2026 Formula 1 Rear Wing Development

CFD Simulation and Development Study

Author

Michele Andreozzi

Published

March 25, 2026

ABSTRACT > This report presents the aerodynamic development of a 2026 Formula 1 rear wing using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations. Seventeen high-downforce configurations were investigated using OpenFOAM, evaluating lift coefficient (\(C_L\)), drag coefficient (\(C_D\)), lift-to-drag ratio (\(L/D\)), and pitching moment (\(C_M\)). The study identifies optimal configurations balancing downforce generation with aerodynamic efficiency.

1 1. Introduction

The 2026 Formula 1 regulations introduce significant changes to rear wing design, emphasizing ground effect and active aerodynamics. This study focuses on CFD-based aerodynamic development of a rear wing configuration, systematically exploring design variations to maximize downforce while maintaining efficient drag characteristics.

The primary objectives of this study are: * Evaluate aerodynamic performance across multiple wing configurations * Identify optimal \(C_L\)/\(C_D\) ratio for maximum race pace performance * Analyze the contribution of pressure and viscous forces to total drag * Assess simulation convergence and numerical accuracy

2 2. Methodology

2.1 2.1 Simulation Setup

All simulations were conducted using OpenFOAM (version v2506) with the following configuration:

  • Solver: simpleFoam (steady-state incompressible)
  • Turbulence Model: k-\(\omega\) SST
  • Mesh Type: Cartesian mesh with refinement zones
  • Boundary Conditions: Velocity inlet, pressure outlet, symmetry planes

2.2 2.2 Geometry

The rear wing geometry consists of a multi-element configuration stored in STL format (rearWing.stl). Each case variation modifies the wing flap angle, element spacing, and Gurney flap configurations to achieve different downforce levels.

The Grid Convergence Index (GCI) was computed using the formula:

\[GCI = F_s \cdot \frac{|f_2 - f_1|}{f_3 - f_1} \cdot \frac{1}{r^3 - 1}\]

where \(F_s = 1.25\) is the safety factor, \(f_1\), \(f_2\), and \(f_3\) are the solutions on coarse, medium, and fine meshes respectively, and \(r\) is the refinement ratio between consecutive meshes.

The refinement ratios are: * \(r_{21} = (N_2/N_1)^{1/3} = (4,953,036/1,490,057)^{1/3} = 1.493\) * \(r_{32} = (N_3/N_2)^{1/3} = (7,709,701/4,953,036)^{1/3} = 1.157\)

2.4 2.4 Simulation

2.4.1 2.4.1 Solver Settings

  • Application: simpleFoam (steady-state incompressible)
  • Turbulence Model: k-\(\omega\) SST
  • Time steps: 500 iterations (steady-state)
  • Freestream velocity: \(U_{\infty} = 50\) m/s
  • Inlet conditions: Fixed velocity inlet
  • Outlet conditions: Pressure outlet (fixed pressure = 0)

2.4.2 2.4.2 Boundary Conditions

  • inlet: Velocity inlet with fixed velocity \(U = (50, 0, 0)\) m/s
  • outlet: Pressure outlet with fixed pressure \(p = 0\) Pa
  • rearWing: No-slip wall condition (\(U = 0\))
  • lowerWall: Fixed velocity (ground plane, \(U = (50, 0, 0)\) m/s - moving ground)
  • symmetry: Symmetry plane at \(y = 0\)
  • outside: Far-field boundary (open)

4 4. Key Findings

  1. Best efficiency: ID_13 achieves the highest L/D ratio of -5.95
  2. Maximum downforce: ID_11 produces the highest downforce with \(C_L\) = -2.96
  3. Minimum drag: ID_03 has the lowest drag coefficient at 0.432
  4. Drag composition: Pressure drag accounts for ~94% of total drag

5 5. Conclusions

The CFD optimization campaign for the 2026 F1 rear wing has successfully identified configurations that balance high downforce with efficient drag characteristics. Future work should focus on validating selected configurations with wind tunnel testing and investigating transient effects.
